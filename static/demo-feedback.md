# Feedback — Price Negotiation (Commercial)

**Scenario:** Price-sensitive commercial customer

---

## What Went Well

- **Acknowledged the price objection:** The salesperson immediately recognized the customer's initial price objection and didn't ignore it.
- **Focused on value over price:** They tried to shift the discussion from "cheapest" to "best," which is fundamentally the right approach for arguing added value.
- **Responded to the warranty concern:** When the customer brought up warranty as an important point, the topic was picked up.

---

## What Could Be Better

- **Be precise with value arguments:** Instead of vague statements like "we're the best" or "the best warranty in our field," use concrete numbers and benefits. The customer asked for "concrete ROI figures and tangible warranty differences" — that's exactly what needed to be delivered.

  > **Suggestion:** "Our **X-year product warranty** and **Y-year performance guarantee** are Z years longer than the industry average. That means greater planning security and lower maintenance costs over the entire lifetime of the system."

- **Proactively provide facts:** The customer *asked* twice explicitly for ROI figures. The salesperson should have had these prepared and presented them actively and convincingly.

  > **Suggestion:** "Mr. Weber, I have concrete ROI calculations for you that show our higher price typically pays for itself after X years through Y, and you achieve Z euros in additional savings over the full term. Shall I walk you through that?"

- **Maintain professionalism:** Ending the conversation abruptly and rudely while the customer is expressing interest is absolutely unacceptable and costs you the deal.

  > **Suggestion:** Even if you feel the customer isn't a fit, stay professional. Instead of hanging up: "Mr. Weber, I sense your concerns. Is there additional information I can provide, or would it make sense to go through the detailed ROI calculations in a follow-up meeting?"

---

## Key Moment

The key moment was when the salesperson ended the conversation with:

> *"You know what, I don't think you're the right customer for us."*

At this point in the conversation, the customer had sent **multiple clear buying signals** by explicitly asking for "concrete ROI figures" and "tangible warranty differences." He even said after being rebuffed: "Wait, I don't give up that easily. I'm definitely interested."

Instead of dismissing him, the salesperson should have presented that information in detail and proposed a next step to further solidify the customer's interest. A golden sales opportunity was destroyed at the last moment.
